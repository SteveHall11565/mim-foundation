The Business Case

Stewardship Isn't a Moral Argument.
It's How You Protect the System That Made You.

Capitalism is under pressure it hasn't faced in 80 years. The leaders who act now — voluntarily, on their own terms — will shape what comes next. The ones who wait will have it shaped for them.

The Threat Is Real

Socialism Doesn't Rise in a Vacuum. It Rises When Capitalism Stops Working for Enough People.

Something important is happening in American politics that business leaders can't afford to ignore. Polling on capitalism vs. socialism has shifted sharply — particularly among voters under 40. States and cities are passing wealth taxes, worker ownership mandates, and AI disclosure laws that would have been unthinkable a decade ago. Federal proposals for windfall taxes on AI profits and mandatory profit-sharing are gaining traction.

This is not a communications problem. It's a performance problem. And the data explains it clearly: labor's share of the economy just hit its lowest point since records began in 1947 — while corporate profits touched their highest since 1950. When the math of capitalism produces those kinds of disparities at scale, the political response is predictable. It's happened before. And when it arrives this time, it will arrive with AI-era speed.

"The choice isn't between stewardship and profit. It's between voluntary stewardship now — or imposed redistribution later."

The consequences of inaction aren't abstract. Higher marginal tax rates. Heavier regulation of AI deployment. Restrictions on M&A and market concentration. Potential nationalization of infrastructure industries. Every one of these outcomes is already in play in multiple legislative chambers. The question is not whether pressure is building. It is whether business leaders act before it breaks.

The Political Risk Dashboard

Higher Taxation

Windfall tax proposals on AI profits already in play in 9 states. Federal minimum corporate tax expanded in 2025 budget.

Heavier Regulation

AI disclosure laws, algorithmic hiring restrictions, and gig worker reclassification mandates spreading state to state.

Nationalization Risk

Public broadband, state-owned AI compute, and Medicare drug pricing are early signals. The frontier moves fast when political will consolidates.

Social License Erosion

Consumer trust in major corporations at a 20-year low. Union organizing up 30% since 2020. The employee social contract is under renegotiation — whether CEOs participate or not.

The AI Imperative

AI Will Either Be the Greatest Uplift in Human History — or the Fastest Wealth Concentration Engine Ever Built. The Difference Is How CEOs Deploy It.

All the money being poured into AI right now is being watched. Workers see it. Consumers see it. Elected officials see it. The question on every stakeholder's mind is: who gets the benefit? Stewardship means CEOs answer that question — before it's answered for them.

AI Stewardship 01

Work Redesign, Not Displacement

The stewardship path for AI is not headcount reduction as the default. It's asking: how do we redeploy our people, redesign their roles, and re-skill them so AI makes them more valuable — not redundant? The companies that answer this well will face no union campaigns, no regulatory blowback, and no recruiting crises.

Companies doing this well: Walmart, Microsoft, Siemens

AI Stewardship 02

Pass the Savings On

AI is driving enormous cost efficiencies. The stewardship question is whether those savings flow exclusively to shareholders — or whether some portion goes back to consumers in lower prices and employees in higher wages. Companies that share the gains build the trust that protects them from political pressure.

The model: Costco, Trader Joe's, Apple's iPhone price trajectory

AI Stewardship 03

Deploy AI for Uplift, Not Just Efficiency

AI has the potential to dramatically lower healthcare costs, improve education outcomes, accelerate drug discovery, and expand access to legal, financial, and mental health services. CEOs who direct AI toward these applications aren't being altruistic — they're building the next great markets.

Opportunity: $500B in healthcare waste. $750B in lost education potential. AI can recover both.

The Stewardship Playbook

Five Things Leaders Can Do Right Now

Stewardship is not a stance. It's a set of operational decisions. Here's what it looks like in practice.

01

Deploy AI to Expand Opportunity, Not Just Reduce Cost

Before any AI automation decision, ask: where can we redeploy these people? What do we have to do to re-skill them? Are we using AI to grow capacity, or just to shrink payroll? The first path builds a durable workforce. The second one builds a political target.

02

Invest the AI Dividend in Product and Workforce, Not Just Buybacks

The savings from AI are real and they're growing. Stewardship means allocating some portion of that dividend to better products for consumers, better wages and benefits for workers, and better infrastructure for communities. This is not charity — it's the investment that maintains the social contract that keeps markets free.

03

Build Resilient Supply Chains That Include, Not Exclude

Geopolitical disruption is real and accelerating. Stewardship means building supply chains designed for resilience and breadth — sourcing from more communities, more domestic suppliers, more small businesses — not just the cheapest global option. The company that builds this now leads every government contracting conversation tomorrow.

04

Invest in Workforce Development as a Strategic Asset

The companies winning the war for talent in 2030 will be the ones that built pipelines — apprenticeships, community college partnerships, re-skilling academies, internal mobility programs — when it was optional. Companies that rely on poaching talent instead of developing it are one AI displacement wave away from a workforce crisis they can't solve.

05

Use Your Platform to Communicate the Positive Story

Business leaders have a credibility and reach that politicians and academics do not. When CEOs show up in their communities, in media, in public policy conversations — with specific examples of how stewardship is working inside their companies — they shift the narrative. Silence on the business case for shared prosperity cedes the story to the people with the loudest grievances.

Behind the Name

Make It Matter

Three words. A challenge and an invitation in equal measure.

The Question

What Does Achievement Now Make Possible?

Every leader in the Make It Matter community has already succeeded — by the conventional scoreboard. They've built companies, generated returns, earned the recognition that comes from winning at the game as it's been played.

They've made it.

The question Make It Matter Foundation asks is the one that follows: so what? What does that achievement now make possible? What does the success you've built now obligate you to do?

The name is a challenge and an invitation simultaneously. Not "you matter" — though that's true. But: make it matter. Take what you've built and deploy it for something larger. Make your leadership the proof of concept for a different kind of capitalism.

The Imperative

"Make It Matter" is not a description of who we are. It is a call to action — directed at every leader who has the power to deploy capitalism differently and is deciding whether to use it.

The leaders who join this foundation haven't failed. They've succeeded. That's precisely what gives the imperative its weight. You have everything you need to make stewardship real. The question is only whether you will.

Three Words

Unpacking the Name

Make

Active. Not "hope for" or "advocate for" — make. Stewardship is not a posture. It's a practice you build into your operating model, your board's agenda, your capital allocation. Make implies agency — and agency is what business leaders have in abundance.

It

Deliberately open. Make what matter? Your leadership. Your company. Your capital. Your influence. Your time. Your legacy. Every leader brings a different "it" — a different portfolio of resources and reach. The foundation's job is to activate all of it in the direction of stewardship.

Matter

The accountability word. Not "make it profitable" — though stewardship produces profitability. Not "make it look good" — though stewardship builds reputation. Matter means: make a genuine difference to workers, customers, communities, and the next generation of leaders you're shaping.

Deep Roots

Stewardship Has a Long History — and a Necessary Future

Our Definition

Stewardship is the active, values-driven management of powerful resources in ways that account for their impact on stakeholders beyond the immediate transaction. It has a long history in domains ranging from natural resource management to institutional governance to fiduciary duty. Applied to business, it means that organizations take genuine responsibility for their consequences — not as a PR posture, but as a core operating principle.

In Natural Resource Management

The steward of a forest doesn't own it — they hold it in trust for the creatures that live in it, the communities that depend on it, the generations who will inherit it. Their job is to leave it healthier than they found it. This is the disposition we're asking business leaders to bring to their organizations.

In Institutional Governance

The steward of a great institution holds authority as a responsibility, not a right — accountable to a purpose larger than themselves. This is the fiduciary imagination Make It Matter Foundation asks business leaders to apply to capitalism itself.

In Business — Right Now

The leaders of America's most powerful companies are stewards of far more than shareholder capital. They are stewards of the social conditions that make capitalism possible — the trust, the legitimacy, the shared belief that the system is worth participating in. When that trust erodes, as it is eroding now, the entire architecture of market capitalism becomes fragile.

Make It Matter Foundation was named for the obligation that follows from understanding this clearly. If you hold the kind of power that shapes how capitalism functions for the people inside your company and around it — then you have the opportunity, and the duty, to make it matter.

The Original Vision

Adam Smith Understood This

Make It Matter Foundation is not building something new. It is recovering something old — the original vision of capitalism as a moral and economic system, in which markets function within a framework of shared values about fairness, reciprocity, and mutual obligation.

Adam Smith wrote both The Wealth of Nations and The Theory of Moral Sentiments — and he did not see them as separate projects. The modern critique of capitalism is not that it is too aligned with Smith's vision. It is that it has strayed from it.

"Stewardship capitalism is not CSR. It is not ESG. It is a return to what capitalism was always supposed to do: create genuine value for customers, employees, and communities — and in doing so, become extraordinarily profitable."

The most profitable path and the most responsible path are the same path, pursued with conviction and community. Make It Matter Foundation exists to prove it.
